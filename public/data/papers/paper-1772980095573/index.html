GENERATIVE REWARD MODELS

Thesis

We propose Generative Reward Models (GenRM), a hybrid approach that unifies Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) and Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback (RLAIF) methodologies by training an LLM on self-generated reasoning traces to produce synthetic preference labels that align with human judgments.

Core Problem

Current alignment approaches (RLHF and RLAIF) face limitations: explicitly parameterized reward models struggle with robustness and generalization to out-of-distribution (OOD) data, and RLAIF methods like LLM-as-a-judge may not always align well with actual user preferences when acting as the sole evaluator.

Key Novelty

A unified Generative Reward Model (GenRM) framework that combines RLHF and RLAIF methodologies, leveraging self-generated reasoning traces and iterative training to fine-tune LLMs for improved preference modeling and alignment, especially for out-of-distribution generalization.

Key Contributions

  • GenRM significantly outperforms zero-shot LLM-based judgments on both in-distribution (9-31% accuracy improvement) and out-of-distribution (2-6% accuracy improvement) tasks.
  • GenRM achieves in-distribution accuracy comparable to Bradley-Terry models while significantly outperforming them on out-of-distribution tasks (10-45% accuracy improvement).
  • STaR-DPO achieves a 91.0% accuracy on the RewardBench Safety category, surpassing the best performing PairRM model (81.8%).
  • On UltraInteract, STaR-DPO achieves 90.2% accuracy for reasoning tasks, significantly outperforming STaR-SFT (68.8%) and other explicit reward models (around 94%).
  • Utilizing inference-time compute, such as majority voting over 32 samples, consistently improves performance by 1.6% on UltraFeedback and 3.8% on RewardBench.

Results

  • Generative Reward Models (GenRM), especially CoT-GenRM and STaR-DPO variants, significantly outperform zero-shot LLM-based judgments and traditional Bradley-Terry reward models on out-of-distribution tasks.
  • Reasoning-based approaches, such as CoT-GenRM and STaR-DPO, show notably stronger performance, particularly on reasoning-heavy and safety-related categories.
  • The STaR-DPO model consistently achieves the strongest results across various benchmarks, demonstrating superior out-of-domain generalization while matching in-distribution performance.
  • Using inference-time compute, such as majority voting, further boosts the accuracy of the critic model, indicating the effectiveness of 'System 2' reasoning approaches.
  • Bootstrapping rationales from stronger models (e.g., GPT-4) can initially underperform standard STaR methods but can be improved with additional on-policy training, highlighting the impact of training methodology on performance.

Limitations

  • A limitation of the current CoT-GenRM approach is the potential for misalignment when incorrect rationales are generated.
  • The training rationales from the post-rationalization model can be off-policy for the base model, leading to a distribution mismatch during training.
  • The STaR-Rationalizer model struggles to generalize to more novel tasks where it generates rationales different from those seen during training.
