Scaling Laws for Reranking in Information Retrieval

Authors: Rahul Seetharaman, Aman Bansal, Hamed Zamani, Kaustubh D. Dhole

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/arXiv:2603.04816

Generated summary artifact for 2603.04816

Thesis

Reranker performance follows predictable power laws with model and data scale, allowing accurate forecasting of large-model performance from smaller-scale training runs and providing a practical approach for efficient planning of expensive reranker training.

Core Problem

While scaling laws are well-established for natural language generation and dense retrieval, they are largely underexplored for reranking systems. Reranking presents unique challenges due to operating on retriever-induced candidate sets, heterogeneous learning-to-rank objectives, and discontinuous top-k metrics like NDCG, making scaling behavior non-obvious.

Key Novelty

First systematic study characterizing scaling laws for rerankers in information retrieval, enabling accurate performance forecasting for large models from smaller-scale experiments across various learning-to-rank objectives.

Key Contributions

  • Reranker performance follows predictable power laws with model size and data scale for metrics like NDCG@10, MAP, and MRR.
  • Accurate forecasting of large-model reranker performance (e.g., NDCG@10 for 1B-parameter models) is possible using only smaller-scale training runs.
  • Scaling behavior (fit quality and apparent exponents) differs across pointwise, pairwise, and listwise fine-tuning objectives.
  • Contrastive Entropy (CE) is less reliable for reranker forecasting compared to NDCG@10 due to sensitivity to score calibration and normalization.

Results

  • Reranker performance follows predictable power laws with model and data scale for traditional metrics like NDCG@10.
  • Accurate forecasting of downstream ranking quality (e.g., NDCG@10) in the large-model regime is possible using only smaller-scale training runs.
  • This provides a practical approach for planning expensive reranker training by reducing the need for full-scale experiments.
  • Scaling behavior (fit quality and apparent exponents) differs across pointwise, pairwise, and listwise objectives, indicating that a single universal fit is unlikely to capture all paradigms equally well.

Limitations

  • Contrastive Entropy (CE) is less reliable for reranker forecasting compared to NDCG@10, as it is sensitive to score calibration and normalization, which can cause fluctuations in trends.
  • Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) does not show predictable scaling on all evaluation datasets, particularly on TREC DL '19.
  • A single universal power-law fit is unlikely to capture the scaling behavior equally well across all pointwise, pairwise, and listwise paradigms due to observed differences in fit quality and exponents.
