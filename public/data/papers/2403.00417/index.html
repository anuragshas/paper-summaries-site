Rethinking Tokenization: Crafting Better Tokenizers for Large Language Models

Authors: Jinbiao Yang

Source: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2403.00417

Generated summary artifact for 2403.00417

Thesis

By drawing inspiration from cognitive science, specifically the 'Principle of Least Effort' (PLE), tokenizers can be designed to autonomously learn an integrated vocabulary of subwords, words, and multiword expressions (MWEs), thereby achieving a more effective balance between the number of tokens and types, leading to more efficient and accurate language processing in LLMs.

Core Problem

Current subword tokenizers (like Byte Pair Encoding, BPE) face limitations in handling non-Latin languages and multiword expressions (MWEs), and rely on extensive training data and computational resources to grasp MWE nuances, impacting Large Language Model (LLM) performance.

Key Novelty

The introduction of the 'Principle of Least Effort' (PLE) from cognitive science as a guiding theoretical framework for tokenizer design, and the development of the Less-is-Better (LiB) model, which learns flexible, cognitively plausible linguistic units (subwords, words, MWEs) to optimize tokenization for LLMs.

Key Contributions

  • LiB achieves the lowest Bits per character (BPC) scores on both English (BRphono) and Chinese (CTB8) corpora, indicating more efficient text representation.
  • LiB demonstrates superior performance even on simple language models, suggesting its units are more cognitively plausible and effective.
  • The model's ability to learn an integrated vocabulary of subwords, words, and Multiword Expressions (MWEs) overcomes limitations of traditional tokenizers, especially for complex languages.
  • LiB's unsupervised method allows it to flexibly adapt to the complexity and diversity of different language inputs while balancing cognitive loads.

Results

  • The LiB model consistently achieves lower Bits per character (BPC) scores across both English and Chinese corpora, demonstrating superior tokenization efficiency compared to Character, BPE subword, and Word tokenizers.
  • LiB autonomously learns an integrated vocabulary comprising subwords, words, and multiword expressions (MWEs), which are more consistent with human cognitive units.
  • The LiB model balances the number of tokens and types effectively, leading to reduced cognitive burden in language processing.
  • This cognitive science-based approach provides a promising new direction for tokenizer design, particularly beneficial for complex languages lacking clear word boundaries like Chinese.

Limitations

  • The paper states that current LiB is primarily a 'cognitive model' and has not been fully optimized for large language models, implying further research is needed for direct LLM integration.
  • The direct introduction of MWEs can increase the number of types significantly (as seen in Jurassic models), potentially contradicting the goal of efficient performance if not carefully selected.
  • Low-frequency MWEs might lead to insufficient representation in training data, making accurate learning and prediction difficult.
