Large Language Models as Annotators for Machine Translation Quality Estimation

Authors: Sidi Wang, Sophie Arnoult, Amir Kamran

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/arXiv:2603.10775

Generated summary artifact for 2603.10775

Thesis

This paper proposes a systematic prompt engineering approach, called PPbMQM, for Large Language Models (LLMs) to generate simplified MQM-style annotations for Machine Translation Quality Estimation. These LLM-generated annotations correlate well with human judgments and can effectively train COMET models to achieve competitive segment-level QE performance for Chinese-English and English-German language pairs.

Core Problem

While Large Language Models (LLMs) demonstrate strong performance in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (MTQE), their high inference costs hinder direct application. The existing Multidimensional Quality Metric (MQM) annotations are complex to operationalize, making it challenging to leverage LLMs effectively for generating high-quality training data for learned QE metrics like COMET.

Key Novelty

A systematic, multi-step prompt engineering approach (PPbMQM) to guide Large Language Models in generating simplified MQM-style annotations for Machine Translation Quality Estimation, incorporating persona, reflection, and output automater patterns, alongside a novel severity scaling method.

Key Contributions

  • GPT-4o, when prompted with PPbMQM, produced annotations that significantly improved span, severity, and error-type F1 metrics compared to its zero-shot performance.
  • COMET models trained on PPbMQM annotations achieved higher Pearson correlation with human annotations than baseline models, especially in lower-quality segments (<0.8 quality score).
  • The synthetic training data generated by PPbMQM enabled COMET models to perform on par with models trained on costly human annotations for Chinese-English and English-German QE tasks.

Results

  • LLMs (especially GPT-4o and LLaMA 3) demonstrate strong knowledge of QE and MQM annotation schemes, outperforming simpler models like GPT-3.5.
  • The proposed few-shot PPbMQM prompting approach, which incorporates detailed error explanations and a severity scaling method, improves span metrics (F1 for span, severity, type) for LLM-generated annotations compared to zero-shot prompting.
  • COMET models trained on PPbMQM-generated annotations achieve competitive performance compared to models trained on human annotations, particularly showing stronger correlation in lower-quality segments for both Chinese-English and English-German.
  • LLMs tend to be stricter annotators than humans, overpredicting errors, but this behavior can be effectively mitigated by implementing a severity scaling method and selectively discarding low-severity annotations.

Limitations

  • Potential data leakage: The test and development datasets used for evaluation might have been included in the training data of the proprietary LLMs, which could inflate performance metrics.
  • Limited domain coverage: The training data for downstream QE models only covered two language pairs (Chinese-English and English-German) and specific domains (news, e-commerce, conversation, social media), limiting generalizability to other domains or language pairs.
  • Single initialization for COMET training: COMET models were trained with only one initialization, which might not be sufficient for robust statistical testing, and future work should test with multiple initializations to strengthen experimental evidence.
