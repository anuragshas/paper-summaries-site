Model Merging in the Era of Large Language Models: Methods, Applications, and Future Directions

Authors: Mingyang Song & Mao Zheng

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/arXiv:2603.09938

Generated summary artifact for 2603.09938

Thesis

This survey provides a comprehensive and structured examination of model merging in the era of Large Language Models (LLMs) through the proposed FUSE taxonomy, bridging theoretical understanding with practical application, to equip researchers and practitioners with a structured foundation for advancing model merging.

Core Problem

The core problem is to efficiently combine the capabilities of multiple neural networks, particularly fine-tuned Large Language Models, into a single unified model without incurring the computational expense of ensembles or full retraining.

Key Novelty

The primary novelty is the introduction of the FUSE taxonomy, a four-dimensional framework (Foundations, Unification Strategies, Scenarios, Ecosystem) that systematically organizes and provides a principled understanding of model merging research in the LLM era.

Key Contributions

  • The FUSE taxonomy provides a structured, four-dimensional framework (Foundations, Unification Strategies, Scenarios, Ecosystem) for understanding model merging research.
  • The survey meticulously details various merging strategies, from simple weight averaging to complex Mixture-of-Experts architectures, elucidating their underlying principles, strengths, and limitations.
  • It highlights the critical importance of shared pretrained initialization and linear mode connectivity as theoretical underpinnings for successful model merging.
  • The paper explores task vector arithmetic as a powerful framework for training-free model editing, enabling intuitive operations such as knowledge addition, negation, scaling, and analogy.
  • It identifies key interference mechanisms (sign conflicts, magnitude disparities, redundant modifications) inherent in task vector arithmetic and discusses sparsification-enhanced methods to mitigate them.
  • The survey compares different merging paradigms based on factors like data-freeness, explicit interference handling, shared initialization requirements, and scalability.

Results

  • Model merging provides a computationally efficient and scalable alternative to ensembles and full retraining for combining specialized LLM capabilities.
  • Shared pretrained initialization and the presence of linear mode connectivity are crucial theoretical underpinnings that enable successful weight interpolation and model merging.
  • A diverse array of unification strategies exists, including weight-space averaging, task vector arithmetic, sparsification-enhanced methods, and Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures, each with distinct benefits and limitations.
  • Model merging finds broad applications across multi-task learning, safety alignment, domain specialization, multilingual transfer, and federated learning scenarios.
  • Key open challenges in the field include theoretical gaps in understanding mergeability at scale, scalability limitations for increasingly large models, and the development of robust predictive frameworks for merge compatibility.
  • The proposed FUSE taxonomy offers a unified organizational framework that can guide future research and practical application in model merging.

Limitations

  • The theoretical understanding of why pretrained LLMs exhibit strong linear mode connectivity after independent fine-tuning on disparate tasks remains incomplete, especially as model scale increases.
  • Predicting merge compatibility requires addressing the computational challenge of efficiently approximating high-dimensional loss surface properties without exhaustive experimentation.
  • Weight-space averaging methods, despite simplicity, often fail when source models are fine-tuned with substantially different learning rates or regularization strengths, causing their average to land on high-loss barriers.
  • Importance-weighted averaging methods introduce computational overhead (e.g., for Fisher Information Matrix estimation) and require access to representative data samples, which may not always be available.
  • Trajectory-based averaging methods typically require access to intermediate checkpoints from a single training run, making them inapplicable when only final model weights are available in open-weight repositories, and can incur significant memory overhead.
  • Geometric interpolation methods are often limited to pairwise merging and can be computationally intensive, sometimes requiring complex alignment problem solving.
  • Task vector arithmetic suffers from parameter interference mechanisms like sign conflicts (opposing directional updates), magnitude disparities (uneven scaling of updates), and redundant parameter modifications (noise accumulation).
  • Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) style merging introduces a memory footprint that scales linearly with the number of experts, making deployment of K-expert systems K-times more expensive than a single merged model.
  • Current theoretical frameworks largely assume exact architectural correspondence, limiting their applicability to scenarios involving models with variations in layer counts or hidden dimensions, posing significant challenges for cross-architecture merging.
