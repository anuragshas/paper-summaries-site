Revisiting Text Ranking in Deep Research

Authors: Chuan Meng, Litu Ou, Sean MacAvaney, Jeff Dalton

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.21456

Generated summary artifact for 2602.21456

Thesis

Existing text ranking methods, including retrievers and re-rankers, can be systematically analyzed and reproduced in the deep research setting using a fixed corpus, revealing key insights into retrieval units, pipeline configurations, and query characteristics. Addressing the training-inference query mismatch through methods like Query-to-Question (Q2Q) is crucial for improving neural ranker performance.

Core Problem

Despite the essential role of search in deep research (which involves multi-hop, reasoning-intensive queries requiring extensive open-web exploration), how existing text ranking methods perform in this setting remains poorly understood due to reliance on black-box live web search APIs and a lack of systematic analysis of search components.

Key Novelty

A systematic, white-box evaluation of diverse text ranking methods in deep research, addressing gaps in understanding retrieval units, re-ranking, and query mismatch, complemented by a novel Query-to-Question (Q2Q) reformulation method.

Key Contributions

  • The lexical retriever BM25, when paired with the gpt-oss-20b agent on the passage corpus, achieved the highest answer accuracy (0.572) across all retrieval settings in the study.
  • The BM25-monoT5-3B pipeline with gpt-oss-20b (re-ranking depth 50) achieved the best overall performance in the study, reaching 0.716 recall and 0.689 accuracy, a configuration comparable to a GPT-5-based agent baseline from prior work.
  • The proposed Query-to-Question (Q2Q) method (Q+R variant) significantly improved neural retriever performance, e.g., SPLADE-v3 accuracy increased by +0.041 and Qwen3-Embed-8B accuracy by +0.042.
  • Re-ranking consistently improved recall and accuracy across all evaluated methods and agents, with deeper re-ranking depths yielding further performance gains and reductions in search calls.
  • Passage-level units enabled higher answer accuracy and completion rates, particularly for agents with smaller context windows (e.g., gpt-oss-20b), by allowing more search and reasoning iterations.

Results

  • Passage-level retrieval units are more efficient for LLM agents with limited context windows, leading to higher answer accuracy and completion rates compared to document-level units, and reducing sensitivity to document length normalization in lexical retrieval.
  • The lexical retriever BM25, when appropriately configured for the deep research setting (e.g., proper document length normalization and indexing only the first 512 tokens), outperforms neural rankers in most cases, especially with web-search-style queries typical of agent interactions.
  • Re-ranking consistently and significantly improves ranking effectiveness and answer accuracy while reducing search calls; these gains are further amplified by deeper re-ranking depths and stronger initial retrievers.
  • The proposed Query-to-Question (Q2Q) method effectively mitigates the training-inference query mismatch for neural rankers, substantially improving their performance, but can harm performance for lexical retrievers like BM25.
  • Reasoning-based re-rankers like Rank1 do not consistently show a clear advantage over non-reasoning ones, potentially due to misinterpretation of keyword-rich web-search queries.

Limitations

  • The study exclusively uses one deep research dataset (BrowseComp-Plus); expanding to other benchmarks with fixed corpora and manual relevance judgments could enhance generalizability.
  • Only two LLMs (gpt-oss-20b and GLM-4.7-Flash) are used as agents; evaluating additional model families and larger model sizes could provide further insights into generalisability.
  • The scope of the study does not include exploring additional rankers or advanced configurations, such as scaling laws.
  • Parameter tuning for BM25 was performed directly on all BrowseComp-Plus queries due to the absence of a dedicated validation set, which warrants caution when comparing tuned BM25 results with other retrievers.
  • The reasoning-based re-ranker Rank1 did not show a clear advantage over non-reasoning methods, sometimes misinterpreting keyword-rich web-search queries, suggesting limitations in its explicit reasoning effectiveness for certain query types.
