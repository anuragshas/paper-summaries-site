Reasoning Models Struggle to Control their Chains of Thought

Authors: Chen Yueh-Han, Robert McCarthy, Bruce W. Lee, He He, Ian Kivlichan, Bowen Baker, Micah Carroll, Tomek Korbak

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/arXiv:2603.05706

Thesis

While Chain-of-Thought (CoT) monitoring is a promising tool for detecting misbehaviors in LLMs, its effectiveness could be undermined if models learn to control their CoT to evade detection. This paper proposes the CoT-Control evaluation suite to measure this 'CoT controllability' and finds that current reasoning models generally exhibit low controllability, which is a positive sign for monitorability, but this capability can be influenced by factors like model scale, reasoning effort, and RL training, suggesting a need for frontier labs to actively track and understand these dynamics.

Core Problem

Chain-of-thought (CoT) monitoring is a promising approach for detecting misbehaviors and understanding the internal reasoning of large language models. However, this approach is vulnerable if models gain the ability to strategically control or 'obfuscate' their CoT to evade detection, thereby undermining oversight mechanisms.

Key Novelty

Introduction of CoT-Control, a novel evaluation suite and metric for measuring the 'CoT controllability' of reasoning models, assessing their ability to strategically modify or suppress their Chain-of-Thought to evade monitoring.

Key Contributions

  • Reasoning models consistently demonstrate significantly lower CoT controllability compared to their ability to control final outputs (e.g., Claude Sonnet 4.5: 2.7% CoT controllability vs. 61.9% output controllability).
  • CoT controllability tends to increase with model parameter size in open-weight models (e.g., GPT-OSS 120B achieved +6.4% higher CoT controllability than GPT-OSS 20B).
  • Increased reasoning effort (longer CoT traces) and more outcome-based Reinforcement Learning (RL) training significantly reduce CoT controllability (e.g., OLMo-3-Think 7B's CoT controllability dropped by 9.5% after RL training).
  • Situational awareness (telling models they are being monitored or evaluated) can slightly increase CoT controllability by up to 4 percentage points, but prompt optimization via automated red-teaming does not reliably generalize to unseen instruction types.
  • Iterative refinement using AI feedback substantially increases CoT controllability (e.g., GPT-OSS 120B showed a +44.0% gain in controllability score after iterations).

Results

  • Reasoning models generally exhibit low CoT controllability, especially when compared to their output controllability, which is a positive indicator for CoT monitorability.
  • Model scale is positively correlated with CoT controllability in open-weight models, suggesting larger models might be more controllable, while increased reasoning effort and RL training on reasoning tasks decrease it.
  • While situational awareness can slightly boost CoT controllability, current adversarial prompt optimization methods are ineffective at reliably increasing generalizable CoT control.
  • The task performance drop is most significant for 'Information Suppression' type of CoT control, indicating a trade-off between control and utility.
  • The ability to iteratively refine CoT compliance via AI feedback significantly enhances controllability, highlighting a potential pathway for improving controllable CoT in models.

Limitations

  • The CoT-Control tasks serve as practical proxies and do not perfectly simulate ideal adversarial settings where models spontaneously pursue misaligned goals and actively try to evade monitors.
  • The study primarily focuses on prompting-based elicitation of CoT controllability, potentially missing other avenues like varying amounts of RL or supervised finetuning that could increase controllability.
  • Models demonstrate better control over certain features of their CoT than others, indicating that the evaluated breadth of control types might be limited.
  • The mechanism behind low CoT controllability is not fully understood, highlighting a need for further research into its causes and implications.
