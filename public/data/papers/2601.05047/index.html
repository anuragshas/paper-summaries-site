📝 Paper Summary

Mixture of Experts (MoE)Reasoning modelsMultimodal LLMsHigh Bandwidth Flash (HBF)Processing-Near-Memory (PNM)
LLM inference requires a radical departure from training-centric hardware designs, prioritizing memory capacity (via HBF), memory bandwidth (via PNM and 3D stacking), and low-latency networking over peak floating-point performance.
Core Problem
LLM inference is in a crisis where hardware designs optimized for compute-heavy training (high FLOPS) are increasingly mismatched with the memory-bound and latency-sensitive nature of the autoregressive Decode phase.
Why it matters:
  • Economic viability of serving LLMs at scale
  • Energy and carbon footprint (CO2e) of datacenters
  • Enabling emerging capabilities like long context, RAG, and multi-step reasoning models
Concrete Example:Serving state-of-the-art models like DeepSeek-v3 with 256 experts or long-context reasoning models is becoming economically unviable due to memory bandwidth bottlenecks and high interconnect latency.
Key Novelty
A pivot in AI hardware research directions from compute-maximization to memory-capacity expansion and low-latency interconnect optimization.
  • Proposes High Bandwidth Flash (HBF) to provide 10X capacity for frozen weights in giant models like MoEs.
  • Distinguishes between PIM (on-die) and PNM (nearby-die), advocating for PNM to handle LLM sharding.
  • Identifies interconnect latency, rather than bandwidth, as the primary network bottleneck for small-batch Decode messages.
Evaluation Highlights
  • HBM bandwidth grew 17X while FLOPS grew 80X between 2012-2022, widening the memory wall.
  • HBM price per GB increased 1.35X from 2023-2025 while DDR prices decreased.
  • High Bandwidth Flash (HBF) can provide 10X memory capacity (512GB vs 48GB) compared to HBM4 stacks.
Breakthrough Assessment
4/5
The paper provides a strategic roadmap for the next decade of AI hardware, transitioning from compute-centric to memory-and-latency-centric designs, authored by a computer architecture pioneer.
